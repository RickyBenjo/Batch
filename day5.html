After-Call Workflow

Core Rule: Record First, Execute Second

Right after every call, submit the Airtable cross-team request form before doing anything else. This keeps a paper trail of what was promised, who's responsible, and what's been completed — so nothing falls through the cracks between calls.

Step-by-Step: After Every Churn Call

1
Go to Airtable → Cross-Team Request FormSelect Partner Experience Team (for all partner support tasks, including after-call work). Select Member Experience Team only for customer/booking issues.
2
Find the Vendor IDGo to the partner's profile page in the Vendor Directory. The ID is in the URL. Copy it directly — don't retype.
3
Fill in Request DetailsState what was offered and accepted. List each task separately: "Listing optimization — all experiences," "Ratings & reviews upload," "Blog post creation," "UGC tool activation," "Media shuffle." Specify which listing(s) if the partner has more than one.
4
Set Priority LevelOn-fire = needs to happen today. High priority = 24–48 hours (use this for after-call tasks — it matches what you told the partner). Low priority = normal queue.
5
Submit + Notify in SlackAfter submitting, tag the specific teammates in Slack with the Airtable request link. Don't just message — create the record first, then notify. Never rely on Slack alone (no paper trail).
6
Assign Directly (Don't Let Teammates Cherry-Pick)In the Airtable request, assign the task to a specific teammate. This prevents confusion, avoidance, and uneven distribution.
7
For Cancellations: Send Intercom Message ImmediatelyUse the Intercom macro (backslash → "cancel" → select PX Cancellation at End of Subscription Term). Edit the billing end date. Send within 2–3 minutes of ending the call. No need to compose from scratch.
8
Prepare for Next CallThe Airtable request should take 1–3 minutes. The Intercom cancellation message should take 2–3 minutes. After both, move straight into pre-call prep for the next partner — 10-minute buffer before each call.

Waiting for Partner vs. In Progress

If you've done your part but the partner hasn't uploaded photos or added reviews yet, mark the field as Waiting for Partner (not yes, not in progress). This lets you come back to these records during slow periods and follow up proactively — "Hey, just checking in, we're still waiting on those photos so we can finalize your optimization."

Airtable Request: Multi-Listing Partners

  • If a partner has multiple listings, always specify which listing(s) the task applies to
  • Example: "Listing optimization — Listing 1: [URL] and Listing 2: [URL]"
  • Without this, teammates may optimize all listings when only one was discussed — wasted work
  • Or worse, they may skip some, assuming someone else will handle it

After-Call Task: Full Execution Checklist (Humpty Key Example)

  • Re-optimize both listings — run CoStar on each, update title, description, tags
  • Copy ratings — use "Add to Listing" button to transfer up to 80 reviews from one listing to the other
  • Send blog post email macro — ask for blog assets (photos, story, key details)
  • Send UGC Collection Tool intro email — use new macro with screenshots and tool link (create this macro if not yet available)
  • Shuffle media — rearrange photos and videos for visual diversity; alternate video and photo; best photo first
  • Notify partner via email — either one consolidated email OR individual emails per offer (your call as supervisor)

Retention Tiers — Updated System

What Changed

The 1-month pause offer has been eliminated. It created extra work (manually pausing/unpausing Stripe) and partners who took it usually churned anyway. Replaced with a structured 3-tier system with more durable retention value.

Tier 1 — Free Modules (Always First)

Activate via the BPM Module checkbox in the partner's Airtable record. These are included in the subscription — you're unlocking them, not adding cost.

Signal StrengthModules to Offer
Strong Signal (5+ itinerary adds)All 3: Content Creator Network + UGC Collection + City Demand Data
Medium Signal (1–4 itinerary adds)All 3: Content Creator Network + UGC Collection + City Demand Data
Weak Signal (0 itinerary adds)City Demand Data only — shows them where the market is heading

Tier 2 — Quarterly-to-Monthly Billing Switch

  • When to offer: Partner cites cash flow concerns, or they are outside their commitment period
  • When NOT to offer: If a partner is still within a commitment period and pays quarterly — switching them to monthly lets them cancel after one month's payment and dispute the rest
  • What it does: Reduces the financial commitment burden without reducing the subscription price
  • How: Change billing cycle in Stripe — don't delete and recreate the subscription

Tier 3 — Temporary Price Reduction (Max 3 Months)

  • Starting offer: 25% discount (e.g., $150/mo → $112.50)
  • Price floor: Never discount a partner already at $99/mo — nothing lower exists
  • Maximum duration: 3 months, tracked in the Pricing Reversion Task tab
  • Discount timing: Ideally offered at the 7-day-before-billing-end meeting, after showing improved metrics — discount reinforces staying, not substitutes for value
  • If they push for more: Escalate to $150/mo flat, then $99/mo — in that order
  • Seasonal partners: If they cancel anyway, advise them to return 3 months before peak season to rebuild SEO and audience
Offer Timing Matters

Don't offer discounts immediately when a partner requests cancellation — it signals desperation and undermines your credibility. The first 6 months of their commitment should be used to prove value and build leverage. Only offer pricing concessions when you have data showing improvement, or when all other levers have been refused.

Stripe Billing & Price Reversion

Why the Old Process Was Broken

The previous workflow deleted the old Stripe product and created a new one at the discounted price. When it was time to revert, there was no record of the original price, and the manual tracking was inconsistent. The new process edits the existing product in-place and uses a dedicated tracking tab.

New Process: Temporary Price Cut in Stripe

1
Find the Partner's Subscription in StripeOpen Stripe → search partner name or email → open their subscription.
2
Edit the Existing Price (Do NOT Delete)Click on the current product price → Edit → change the amount directly (e.g., $250 → $187.50 for 25% off). Do not delete the product and add a new one.
3
Schedule the ChangeApply on a custom date — set it to the end of the current billing cycle so they start the discount on their next bill, not mid-cycle.
4
Log in the Pricing Reversion Task TabIn the Google Sheet, record: partner name, original price, discounted price, start date, and reversion date (3 months out). This is your reminder to undo the discount.
5
Email Partner ~1 Week Before ReversionSend a heads-up: "Just a reminder that your promotional rate ends on [date] and your subscription will return to $[original] on [date]. Here's what we've accomplished together in the last 3 months…" Frame it as a natural transition, not a price hike.
6
On Reversion Date: Edit Price BackRepeat step 2 in reverse — edit the price back to original. Check the Pricing Reversion Task tab weekly so nothing is missed.
⚠️ Never Delete & Re-Add a Stripe Product

Deleting the product creates a new subscription record, loses billing history context, and makes it harder to track reversion. Always edit the existing price in place. The paper trail stays intact and reversions are cleaner.

Cancellation in Stripe — Quick Recap

1

2
Always choose "End of Current Period"Never cancel immediately unless partner specifically demands it and you've confirmed with Stefan. They paid for the time — they keep it.

4

Dispute Management

Tool: Batch Dispute Evidence Builder

Use the Evidence Builder before deciding whether to counter or accept a dispute. It assesses win probability based on the type of dispute, time elapsed, services provided, and documentation available. Only counter if the evidence supports it — accepting a losing dispute is better than submitting weak evidence.

Process: When a Partner Files a Dispute

1
Run the Dispute Evidence BuilderAssess win probability. Identify the dispute type (fraudulent, product not received, services not rendered, etc.).
2
Call the Partner FirstBefore submitting any counter evidence, call the partner directly. Request that they withdraw the dispute. Offer support for their underlying concerns. Most disputes are really retention problems in disguise.
3a
If Partner Withdraws the DisputeConfirm via email immediately. Document the withdrawal. Proceed with retention offers as normal.
3b
If Partner Refuses and Wants to CancelCancel their account but inform them you will still counter the dispute, citing Batch Partner Policies. Don't let a cancellation mean you forfeit the dispute.
3c
If Partner Refuses but Doesn't CancelCounter the dispute using the Evidence Builder output. Submit all relevant documentation: listing activity, communications history, billing records, services rendered.
4
Add a Detailed Note in StripeRegardless of outcome, add a note explaining the decision — accepted or countered, and why. This creates a paper trail for Stefan and any future disputes from the same partner.

Case Study
Morena Miami Tan — Accepted Dispute

The Situation

Partner disputed a $200 charge as "fraudulent." Investigation revealed they had paid for 5 months (~$1,000 total) before their listing went live on April 29. The disputed charge was from April 11 — 18 days before their listing was even published.

The Decision: Accept the Dispute

Rationale: Batch failed to deliver the onboarding/support service they were paying for during that 18-day window. The partner received no value from that specific charge, making the refund justified — even though they technically labelled it "fraudulent." Accepting was the right call morally and legally.

Lesson

The dispute label doesn't always match the underlying issue. Look at what actually happened: did we provide the service? If no — accept. If yes — counter with evidence.

What "Win Probability" Actually Means

  • High win probability: You have listing activity records, email logs, optimization history, and the partner was active and using the platform
  • Low win probability: Partner never had their listing live, there's minimal communication history, or there's evidence of a service failure on Batch's side
  • The current win rate is ~6% — this is very low and signals that disputes aren't being handled with enough documentation upfront
  • Adding Stripe notes after every call is one of the key ways to build better dispute evidence going forward

Batch Partner Policies

Where to Find Them

Admin Panel → Submit Service Request → click the "Batch Partner Policies" hyperlink. Review these before any dispute or commitment enforcement conversation.

Key Policy Clauses

PolicyWhat It MeansHow to Use It
14-Day Cancellation Notice Partners must give 14 days' notice before cancelling Powerful tool to gain time for retention efforts. "Per our partner policy, we do require 14 days' notice — which gives me time to put together a proper optimization plan for you before your listing deactivates."
Commitment Terms (3/6/12 months) Partners agreed to a minimum commitment period when signing up Reframe as an SEO investment: "The reason for the commitment period is that SEO takes 3 months to compound. We want to make sure you've had enough time to actually see results before making a decision." Never weaponize it — use it as context.
No Refund Policy Default is no refunds on subscription payments This is the baseline — but the retention playbook overrides it (prorated refunds approved by Stefan for special cases). Never promise refunds without Stefan approval.
Non-Disclosure / Confidentiality Partners agreed not to disclose platform details Rarely enforced due to lack of legal resources — but exists in the policy. Reference only if partner is publicly disparaging the platform.

Enforcing the 12-Month Commitment — When to Use It

  • Use it once: Enforce the commitment on the first call. Schedule the cancellation for the end of the commitment term and fill that window with high-value optimizations.
  • Don't use it twice: If a partner has already tried to cancel before and you enforced the policy then, do not enforce it again. Process the cancellation immediately — otherwise it looks predatory.
  • High frustration = immediate cancel: If a partner is extremely upset or claims they were misled, processing the cancellation immediately (even within commitment) is better than enforcing a policy and destroying the relationship or risking a dispute.
  • Enforcement creates a window: A 6-month remaining commitment = 6 months to demonstrate value, convert them to bookable, and prevent the eventual churn.
⚠️ Refunds Always Require Stefan Approval

Never promise a refund on a call without Stefan's sign-off. On the call, say: "I'll submit that to our team for review and follow up with you within 24–48 hours regarding the decision." Don't say "I'll see what I can do" — it sounds like a yes that may not come through.

Live Call Case Studies

Context: Live Shadowing

These are real churn calls handled on May 1. Each illustrates a different scenario and decision framework. Study the reasoning behind each outcome — not just what was done, but why.

Churned
Blumonsense — Immediate Cancellation

The Situation

Core partner at $105.33/month with 3 Fair Harbor listings. Cited "no ROI" and "too expensive." Zero bookings in 435 days. Profile was incomplete — no logo, missing reviews and videos. Also had a prior cancellation request in July 2025.

Decision: Process Immediate Cancellation (May 18)

The prior cancellation request was the key signal — this partner had already mentally checked out almost a year ago and was pushed to stay. A hard-save attempt would come across as predatory. Respect the decision. Scheduled for May 18 (end of current period).

Lesson

Prior cancellation attempts signal a firm decision. Don't fight it twice — it damages the relationship and wastes call time. Send the cancellation confirmation email with Batch Partner Policy reference (no refunds clause).

Saved (6-Month Window)
Camp Tiki — Churn Delay & Optimization

The Situation

Partner at $150/month on a 12-month commitment. Requested cancellation immediately after a $825 semi-annual payment (with $75 discount already applied). Cited "too expensive" and "not seeing enough ROI." Cancellation requested just after a large payment — emotional, not purely rational.

Decision: Enforce Commitment, Schedule for October 31

Still within the 12-month term. Enforced the commitment — cancellation scheduled for end of period (Oct 31). To justify the remaining 6 months, immediately offered a full, high-value optimization package:

  • Copy 80 reviews from one listing to another
  • Reshuffle media to refresh content
  • Activate UGC Collection Tool (QR codes, prizes)
  • Create a blog post
  • Re-optimize listings with new keywords

Outcome

Partner accepted the plan. 6-month window created to demonstrate value. Schedule 30/60/90-day follow-ups to track progress and build the case for renewal.

Lesson

Enforce the commitment once — and immediately back it up with action. The commitment gives you time; the optimization package gives you a reason to use it.

Churned (Waived Commitment)
Borgz — Immediate Cancellation

The Situation

Partner at $99/month on a 12-month commitment. Claimed a misunderstanding about the monthly payment structure — said they didn't know they'd be billed monthly for 12 months. Profile incomplete (no videos, no reviews). Weak signal: zero impressions.

Decision: Immediate Cancellation (May 29) — Commitment Waived

Zero impressions means no SEO traction at all — there's nothing to point to. The "misled" claim, even if not fully accurate, made enforcing the policy too risky. A partner who feels deceived will file a dispute. Processing the cancellation immediately cost less than a potential dispute loss.

Lesson

When a partner claims they were misled and there's genuinely no performance data to show them, enforcing the commitment makes Batch look predatory. Cut the loss cleanly. Send the cancellation confirmation email (Katelyn).
